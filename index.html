The 4 reference lines

Ideal, Executed, Faded, Blowup

Every chart compares against these four benchmarks. Here is exactly what each one means.

✦

Ideal

The engineering target

Not a group of runners — a mathematical benchmark. The grade-adjusted optimal pace per segment from the official Boston elevation profile. It answers: what would your pace be if you ran perfectly even effort across every elevation change? Running faster than ideal in Seg.1 means borrowing from later miles.

Executed (~33%)

The best-paced third

Runners whose positive split falls in the bottom third of their bucket — smallest gap between first and second half. Not necessarily the fastest, but the best-matched to the course. Defining trait: they go slower than ideal in Seg.1. They refuse the free downhill speed. That restraint buys a strong Seg.4.

Faded (~34%)

The middle third

Runners in the middle third of positive splits for their bucket. Started slightly faster than ideal in Seg.1 — not recklessly, but enough to accumulate debt. Second half slower but not catastrophic. The most common Boston experience: controlled until mile 20, then required significant effort to hold pace through Newton Hills.

Blowup (~33%)

The top-split third

Runners in the top third of positive splits — largest first/second half gap. Went 6–10 sec/km faster than ideal in Seg.1, felt strong through Seg.2, and hit the Newton Hills with an empty glycogen tank. Seg.4 is 13–25 sec/km worse than ideal. These are not bad runners — they made one decision in Hopkinton and that decision decided their race.

Methodology

How the analysis works

A full engineering breakdown of every analytical decision made in this study.

1 — Data source

Official BAA chip timing results for 2023 and 2025. Each runner has splits at 9 checkpoints: 5K, 10K, 15K, 20K, Half, 25K, 30K, 35K, 40K, Finish. ~26,600 finishers in 2023 and ~28,400 in 2025. Men and women analyzed separately.

2 — Time buckets

Runners grouped into ±2:30 windows around each target time. The '2:50' bucket = all runners finishing between 2:47:30–2:52:30. Above 4:00, buckets widen to ±7:30 as the field thins. Men start at 2:20; women at 2:55.

3 — Outlier removal

Any runner with a single-segment deviation >±15% from their own average is removed (timing errors, medical stops, extreme blowups). Removal: 3.6% at 2:40 → 37.9% at 5:00. All metrics use median, not mean — robust to the right-skewed positive split distribution.

4 — Group classification

After outlier removal, every runner is classified by their positive split. Thresholds = 33rd/67th percentile within each bucket — so 'Executed' always means the best-paced third of that specific bucket. Result: ~33% Executed / ~34% Faded / ~33% Blowup, stable across every bucket and both genders.

5 — Grade-adjusted ideal pacing

Ideal pace uses the Runners Connect grade-adjusted formula on official Boston mile-by-mile elevation. +13 sec/mi per 1% uphill (above +0.4% threshold); −8 sec/mi per 1% downhill (below −1.5%). A base flat pace B is solved algebraically so all adjusted segment times sum exactly to the goal time. Ideal is not a prediction — it is the engineering benchmark.

6 — Why 2024 is excluded

2024 was run at 69°F (21°C) with sustained east headwind. Positive splits were nearly double 2023 across all buckets (e.g. men 2:50: +2:49 in 2023 vs +6:23 in 2024 — same runners, same course, 134% more second-half degradation). Including 2024 inflates all deviation metrics and misrepresents normal Boston conditions.

The grade-adjusted ideal formula — derivation

For mile m: grade G_m = Δelev / 1609.34 × 100%. Adjustment A_m = +13×G_m if G_m > 0.4%, −8×G_m if G_m < −1.5%, else 0 (sec/mi). Target pace P_m = B + A_m. Constraint: Σ(P_m × d_m) = T_goal → B = (T_goal − Σ(A_m × d_m)) / 26.2. Segment ideal = distance-weighted mean of P_m over each segment, converted to sec/km.

Elevation: official Boston mile markers (Daniels/Burfoot). Verified against Runners Connect calculator output for multiple goal times.
